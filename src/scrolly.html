For centuries, Columbia has prided itself on linking students and scholars from across the world to the “vast resources of a great metropolis”: New York City. But with the University’s growth, the city has presented a less glamorous reality: debilitating space constraints.

From the day of his inauguration, former University President Lee Bollinger, Law ’71, sought to solve this issue. He made Columbia’s expansion into its surrounding community a central focus of his 21-year tenure, championing the controversial construction of the 17-acre, $6.3 billion Manhattanville campus.

As Columbia erected expensive new facilities for its professional schools in Manhattanville, just ten blocks south, the Faculty of Arts and Sciences—which calls itself the intellectual heart” of the University—languished.

FAS bore large portions of the construction costs for the new campus. However, the University allocated minimal space for FAS, which oversees nearly half the student body, in Manhattanville itself.

So, in 2006, Bollinger promised Uris Hall—then home to the Business School—to FAS. Sitting at the center of the Morningside campus, Columbia expected Uris to increase the space available for FAS by about 20 percent. But to become fully usable, Uris needed a full-scale renovation—one that, under the University’s financial structure, FAS would have to fund on its own.

By 2021, Jonathan Lavine, CC ’88, then co-chair of the board of trustees, pledged $40 million for the renovation, and Columbia’s central administration promised to match it. With an $80 million budget, the FAS started planning for what would become known as the Lavine Center. A year later, the Business School finally moved out of Uris and into its $600 million new home in Manhattanville.

As departments and student groups began competing for space in the renovated building, FAS set the Lavine Center’s expected opening for fall 2025.

But construction on the Lavine Center never started.
