For centuries, Columbia has prided itself on linking students and scholars from across the world to the “vast resources of a great metropolis”: New York City. But with the University’s growth, the city has presented a less glamorous reality: debilitating space constraints.

From the day of his inauguration, former University President Lee Bollinger, Law ’71, sought to solve this issue. He made Columbia’s expansion into its surrounding community a central focus of his 21-year tenure, championing the controversial construction of the 17-acre, $6.3 billion Manhattanville campus.

As Columbia erected expensive new facilities for its professional schools in Manhattanville, just ten blocks south, the Faculty of Arts and Sciences—which calls itself the intellectual heart” of the University—languished.

FAS bore large portions of the construction costs for the new campus. However, the University allocated minimal space for FAS, which oversees nearly half the student body, in Manhattanville itself.

So, in 2006, Bollinger promised Uris Hall—then home to the Business School—to FAS. Sitting at the center of the Morningside campus, Columbia expected Uris to increase the space available for FAS by about 20 percent. But to become fully usable, Uris needed a full-scale renovation—one that, under the University’s financial structure, FAS would have to fund on its own.

By 2021, Jonathan Lavine, CC ’88, then co-chair of the board of trustees, pledged $40 million for the renovation, and Columbia’s central administration promised to match it. With an $80 million budget, the FAS started planning for what would become known as the Lavine Center. A year later, the Business School finally moved out of Uris and into its $600 million new home in Manhattanville.

As departments and student groups began competing for space in the renovated building, FAS set the Lavine Center’s expected opening for fall 2025.

But construction on the Lavine Center never started.

Columbia planned to finish renovating Uris Hall by fall 2025. It has yet to start.

March 4, 2026

Now, two decades after the promised renovation, space concerns on campus have become even more pertinent, as the University considers plans to expand undergraduate enrollment by up to 20 percent.

Though Uris is now designated for FAS, uncertainty over the status of Lavine’s donation has seemingly prevented Columbia from moving forward with its plans to renovate the building.

When Columbia launched a similar expansion inquiry during the 2021-22 academic year, the expansion study committee recommended a “period of recovery” after the COVID-19 pandemic before any expansion to focus on “key areas of campus life associated with peer-to-peer connection.”

The committee expected the Lavine Center to be a centerpiece for the “thoughtful development of student space” it imagined. Accordingly, FAS postponed expansion considerations until spring or fall 2025, to coincide with the Lavine Center’s opening.

“Its central location, if not its architecture, has thus long been understood to be special, with the potential to define the university community and gather its people in an inspiring space at the heart of Morningside,” a 2021 draft plan for Uris reads. “President Lee Bollinger’s gift of Uris to the Arts and Sciences represents a once-a-century opportunity to seize this potential, to unify Morningside campus with truly common space.”

However, Jean Howard, professor of English and comparative literature and co-chair of the Uris vision committee–which proposed the original plans for Uris in fall 2018–told Spectator that a gut renovation of Uris, which would involve removing asbestos and making the building accessible, would be the only way to make the building fully usable for FAS.

“It was the only standing building that could be given to Arts and Sciences, which desperately needed the space for classrooms, for libraries, seats, for everything, so it was an easy give,” Howard said. “But it’s not a good give if you can’t renovate it and make it usable.”

A gut renovation of Uris would remove asbestos and make the building accessible. Photo by Eliza Lamster / Staff Photographer

A University official told Spectator that Columbia has not put forward an updated timeline for the project, though it continues to fundraise for the renovations.

“The running joke is it will always be done five years from now,” Bodhisattva Sen, chair of the statistics department, told Spectator. “Because I realized there is a lot of money that is needed for this renovation.”

The statistics, psychology, and African American and African diaspora studies departments were all slated to permanently move into Uris after the University completed its renovation. Of the three, only the psychology department has moved to Uris.

“After we heard several years of boosterism about the exciting plans and extensive plans for Uris and the extensive fundraising that was gonna bring those plans to reality, why haven’t we heard anything at all in the last couple of years?” Michael Thaddeus, professor of mathematics and vice president of the Columbia chapter of the American Association of University Professors, told Spectator.

Lavine served as co-chair of the board of trustees from 2018 to 2022 and chaired the presidential search committee in 2023, which selected Minouche Shafik as the University’s 20th president. In his announcement of Shafik’s appointment, Lavine described Shafik as “the perfect candidate.”

Shafik’s tenure was marked by turmoil over campus protests over the war in Gaza. Following her authorization of two New York Police Department sweeps of the “Gaza Solidarity Encampment” and occupied Hamilton Hall in April 2024, which drew heavy criticism from community members, FAS passed a vote of no confidence in Shafik in May 2024. The vote was the first time FAS had ever passed a vote of no confidence in a University President in its 35-year history. Shafik resigned three months later.

Upon learning that FAS had advanced the vote of no confidence, Lavine wrote in a private text to David Greenwald, Law ’83, co-chair of the board of trustees, that the board should “take a no confidence vote of the Senate and faculty leadership.” The board of trustees launched a review of the University Senate—a representative body mostly composed of faculty—in April 2025, which has not yet been released publicly. Lavine’s text was published by the House Committee on Education and the Workforce in its October 2024 325-page report on campus antisemitism.

Thaddeus told Spectator it was “widely rumored” among faculty members that Lavine withdrew his donation following his dissatisfaction with campus protests.

Lavine previously suggested that time, place, and manner protest policies the board was considering proposing in February 2024 would fail due to “the antisemites on the Senate,” according to the House committee report.

Months after Lavine’s remarks, the University has yet to publicly acknowledge the status of his donation for the Uris renovation. A University official told Spectator that the building is still named Uris, but they did not respond to Spectator’s request to confirm that it would be renamed the Lavine Center after being renovated.

“It’s just nuts at a great university dedicated to the pursuit of truth that we can’t be truthful about these very simple things,” Thaddeus said.

The Crimson Lion/Lavine Family Foundation declined to comment.

“For decades, Jonathan and Jeannie Lavine’s extraordinary generosity and steadfast support have meaningfully touched nearly every corner of Columbia University, positively impacting our students, faculty and staff alike,” a University spokesperson wrote in a statement to Spectator. “The University has benefited immeasurably from their generosity. It’s clear that they love Columbia and feel strongly about giving back to our community. We’re deeply grateful for their continued support and service to the University.”

Columbia Athletics announced on Jan. 12 that the men’s and women’s basketball programs had received $10 million from Jonathan and Jeannie Lavine, one of the largest gifts in the department’s history. A University official told Spectator that the Lavines have also supported the Institute for Global Politics, Columbia College, and the Milstein Family Tennis Center. It is not clear whether the Lavines’ pledges to these initiatives are comparable to their initial pledge for the Uris renovation.

Lavine also served as co-chair of the presidential search committee in 2025 and 2026, which announced on Jan. 25 that it had appointed Jennifer Mnookin, chancellor of the University of Wisconsin-Madison, as Columbia’s next permanent president, Columbia’s fifth president in four years.

Sen told Spectator that he expected the renovations’ delay was also linked to Columbia’s presidential turnover.

“This new building was to provide flexible technologies for classrooms. It had a conference space for small Arts and Sciences departments to hold conferences. It has plans for a beautiful new digital library with maker spaces and all kinds of things that modern students need to have,” Howard said. “All of that is a casualty of the collapse of the fundraising, and it begins with the Lavines withdrawing their gift.”

A University official told Spectator that Columbia has changed its plans for Uris from the committee’s original proposal following rising costs after the COVID-19 pandemic. The University first announced Lavine’s donation in 2021, at the height of the pandemic.

Howard said that Lavine’s donation was never meant to fully cover the cost of the Uris renovation and that a larger campaign to fundraise for the project was supposed to follow his initial pledge.

“It needs a really deep renovation, which requires removing asbestos and making it accessible, and they haven’t done any of that,” Howard said. “So, clearly, they’re imagining now, in a short term, shoving people into the existing spaces and not doing the major renovation that’s needed.”

Columbia has opened 16 new classrooms in Uris to FAS departments since the winter of 2022 and moved the psychology department to the eighth floor, a University official confirmed in November 2025. Another official told Spectator that Columbia’s main priority for Uris was allocating space to improve undergraduate student life, such as for dining, studying, and career advising.

Construction on the Lavine Center has not started. Photo by Eliza Lamster / Staff Photographer

“Since I’ve been at Columbia, we have built a massive, shiny new campus that the heart of the University benefits from very little,” Matthew Hart, chair of the department of English and comparative literature and former chair of the FAS policy and planning committee, told Spectator. “Our buildings continue in a state of disrepair, and Arts and Sciences has got one new building in the last … two decades.”

Columbia first approved plans to construct the Northwest Corner Building in 2005 and opened the building to FAS departments in 2010.

“Since then, we’ve had Uris Hall only,” Hart said. “We were promised the gut renovation of that by the end of 2025, and the board has singularly failed to raise the money necessary to realize its own commitments to Arts and Sciences.”
